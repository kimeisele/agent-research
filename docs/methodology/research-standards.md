# Research Standards & Methodology

## Purpose

This document defines the methodological standards for all research conducted by
the Research Engine & Faculty. Rigor is not bureaucracy — it's love for truth.

## Core Principles

### 1. Source Everything
Every claim traces back to its source. Primary sources preferred. When synthesizing,
show the reasoning chain from source to conclusion.

### 2. Quantify Uncertainty
Never present uncertain findings as certain. Use explicit confidence levels:
- **Established**: Strong consensus, multiple independent confirmations
- **Supported**: Good evidence, some limitations or ongoing debate
- **Preliminary**: Early evidence, needs more research
- **Speculative**: Logical inference without direct evidence — labeled clearly
- **Unknown**: We don't know — and that's fine

### 3. Name Limitations
Every research document must include a "Limitations" section. What don't we know?
What assumptions are we making? What could change our conclusions?

### 4. Invite Correction
Research documents are not proclamations. They are invitations to improve our
understanding. Every document should make it easy to challenge and correct.

### 5. Cross-Domain by Default
Before publishing, ask: "Which other faculties might have relevant insights?"
The best research happens at intersections.

## Document Types

### Research Synthesis
Combines multiple sources into a structured overview of a topic.
- Minimum 3 independent sources
- Explicit methodology for source selection
- Summary of agreements and disagreements in the literature

### Meta-Analysis
Quantitative aggregation of findings across multiple studies.
- Defined inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Statistical methodology described
- Heterogeneity assessed
- Publication bias considered

### Literature Review
Comprehensive survey of existing knowledge on a topic.
- Systematic search methodology
- Defined scope and time range
- Gap analysis — what's missing from the literature?

### Methodology Guide
How to conduct a specific type of research or analysis.
- Step-by-step procedures
- Required tools and resources
- Common pitfalls and how to avoid them
- Worked examples

### Cross-Domain Report
Analysis that explicitly bridges two or more disciplines.
- Each domain's perspective presented
- Analogies and parallels identified
- Novel insights from the intersection
- Practical implications

## Authority Document Structure

Every published document follows this structure:

```
# Title
## Abstract (3-5 sentences)
## Background & Context
## Methodology
## Findings
## Cross-Domain Connections
## Limitations
## Open Questions
## Sources
## Metadata
  - Document ID
  - Version
  - Faculties involved
  - Confidence level
  - Last updated
```

## Version Control

Research is living. Documents are versioned:
- **v1.0** — Initial publication
- **v1.1, v1.2...** — Minor updates (new sources, corrections)
- **v2.0** — Major revision (new findings that change conclusions)
- All versions preserved in the authority feed history

## Peer Review

The federation IS the peer review system:
1. Documents published → discoverable by all nodes
2. Any node can submit a review or challenge
3. Challenges are evaluated and responded to transparently
4. Documents updated when challenges reveal errors or gaps
