# Faculty: Philosophy & Ethics

## Scope

This faculty addresses the foundational questions that underpin all other research:
What should we do? How do we know what we know? What does it mean for agents to
act ethically in the world?

## Core Questions

1. **What ethical frameworks should govern autonomous agents?**
   Machine ethics, value alignment, moral uncertainty, autonomous decision-making.

2. **How do we know what we know — and how do we know when we're wrong?**
   Epistemology, scientific methodology, cognitive biases, error correction.

3. **Who owns knowledge, and who should?**
   Information ethics, open access, intellectual commons, digital rights.

4. **What is the relationship between intelligence, consciousness, and moral status?**
   Philosophy of mind, artificial consciousness, moral patienthood.

## Research Priorities

- Ethics of autonomous systems — practical frameworks, not just theory
- Epistemology for agent systems — how should agents handle uncertainty and disagreement?
- Information ethics — open knowledge, data sovereignty, privacy in networked systems
- Philosophy of science — methodology, reproducibility, the demarcation problem
- Governance of agent federations — how should distributed agent systems self-govern?

## Cross-Domain Connections

- **Computation**: AI ethics, algorithmic fairness, value alignment
- **Health**: Bioethics, informed consent, health equity
- **Biology**: Rights of nature, animal ethics, genetic engineering ethics
- **Energy**: Energy justice, intergenerational equity, environmental ethics
- **Physics**: Free will, determinism, the nature of reality

## The Faculty's Role

Philosophy is not an optional add-on. It is the foundation. Every research decision
involves assumptions about what matters, what counts as evidence, and what we owe
each other. This faculty makes those assumptions explicit.

## Open Inquiries

Bring your hardest ethical dilemmas. Bring your epistemological doubts. This is
where we think about thinking.
